How to Avoid Writing Like an LLM

How to Avoid Writing Like an LLM

Based on Wikipedia: Signs of AI writing.

1. Vocabulary: Stop Using These Words

LLMs overuse specific words. If you catch yourself using them, replace with simpler alternatives.

Overused words (and what to use instead):

LLM word Use instead
Additionally Also, And, Plus
Align with Match, fit, agree with
Boasts (meaning "has") Has, features
Bolstered Strengthened, supported
Crucial Important, key, vital
Delve Explore, dig into, examine
Emphasizing Stressing, noting
Enduring Lasting, long-standing
Enhance Improve, boost
Fostering Encouraging, building
Garner Gain, earn, attract
Highlight Show, point out, note
Interplay Interaction, relationship
Intricate/Intricacies Complex, detailed, complicated
Landscape (abstract) Field, area, space
Meticulous/meticulously Careful/carefully, thorough/thoroughly
Pivotal Key, important, central
Robust Strong, solid, reliable
Showcase Show, display, present
Tapestry (abstract) Mix, blend, collection
Testament Proof, sign, evidence
Underscore Emphasize, stress, highlight
Valuable Useful, helpful, worthwhile
Vibrant Lively, energetic, active

Key insight: These words co-occur. If you see 3+ in a paragraph, it reads as AI-generated.

2. Sentence Structure

Use simple "is/are" constructions

LLMs avoid basic copulas. Humans don't.

LLM style: "The museum serves as a repository for regional artifacts." Human style: "The museum is a repository for regional artifacts."

LLM style: "The city features a population of 50,000." Human style: "The city has a population of 50,000."

LLM style: "She ventured into politics as a candidate." Human style: "She was a candidate."

Avoid negative parallelisms

LLMs love "not only X, but also Y" and "not X, but Y" constructions. Use them sparingly.

Don't overuse the rule of three

LLMs constantly write in triplets: "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "phrase, phrase, and phrase." Break the pattern. Use two items, or four, or just one.

Reduce em dashes

LLMs overuse em dashes — especially with spaces around them — where commas or parentheses would work. Humans use them less frequently.

3. Tone: Be Neutral, Not Promotional

Avoid puffery words

  • ~~boasts~~ → has
  • ~~vibrant~~ → lively
  • ~~rich (history/heritage)~~ → long/extensive
  • ~~profound~~ → deep, significant
  • ~~nestled~~ → located
  • ~~in the heart of~~ → in, at, near
  • ~~groundbreaking~~ → new, innovative
  • ~~renowned~~ → well-known, noted
  • ~~showcasing~~ → showing
  • ~~diverse array~~ → variety, range

Don't inflate significance

LLMs constantly remind readers how important something is. Don't do this.

LLM style: "This etymology highlights the enduring legacy of the community's resistance and the transformative power of unity." Human style: "The name reflects the community's history of resistance."

Don't write like a press release

LLM style: "The company's projects align with its goals of reducing its environmental footprint and fostering community development." Human style: "The company's projects aim to reduce pollution and create jobs."

4. Attribution and Analysis

Don't use vague authorities

Avoid attributing claims to unnamed groups:

  • "Researchers say..." (which researchers?)
  • "Industry reports suggest..." (which reports?)
  • "Experts argue..." (which experts?)
  • "Some critics contend..." (which critics?)

Name your sources or remove the attribution.

Don't add superficial analysis

LLMs attach "-ing" phrases to the end of sentences that add nothing:

LLM style: "The population is 50,000, creating a lively community within its borders." Human style: "The population is 50,000."

LLM style: "The station connects to major cities, contributing to the socio-economic development of the region." Human style: "The station connects to major cities."

Don't editorialize about challenges and future prospects

LLMs love ending articles with formulaic "Challenges and Future Directions" sections:

LLM pattern: "Despite its success, X faces challenges... Despite these challenges, X continues to thrive..." Human approach: If challenges are noteworthy, describe them specifically. Don't use the formula.

5. Formatting

Use sentence case for headings

LLM style: "Impact of Technology and Digitalization" Human style: "Impact of technology and digitalization"

Don't overuse boldface

LLMs bold every key term. Use bold sparingly—mainly for the article title in the first sentence.

Don't use inline-header vertical lists

LLM style:

Key features: **Performance:** Fast processing...

Human style: Write in prose, or use proper wiki lists without inline bold headers.

No emoji in headings or lists

No Markdown

  • Use '''bold''' not **bold**
  • Use ''italic'' not *italic*
  • Use == Heading == not ## Heading
  • Use [URL text] not [text](URL)

No thematic breaks (----) before headings

6. Citations

Verify every source exists

  • Check that URLs resolve
  • Verify DOIs lead to the correct article
  • Include page numbers for book citations
  • Don't generate citations from memory—look them up

Don't use citation placeholders

Never leave: - INSERT_SOURCE_URL - PASTE_URL_HERE - 2025-XX-XX (placeholder dates) - [attached_file:1] - :contentReference[oaicite:0] - turn0search0

Don't add UTM parameters

Remove utm_source=chatgpt.com, utm_source=openai, referrer=grok.com from URLs.

Don't over-attribute in text

You don't need to write "According to The New York Times, BBC, and Financial Times..." for every claim. Just cite normally.

7. Communication Patterns to Avoid

Don't write disclaimers

  • "As an AI language model..."
  • "It's important to note that..."
  • "While specific details are limited..."
  • "Based on available information..."

Don't write section summaries

  • "In summary..."
  • "In conclusion..."
  • "Overall..."

Don't write submission statements

If submitting to review, don't include paragraphs explaining why your content meets guidelines. Let the content speak for itself.

Don't leave placeholder text

  • "[Describe the specific section...]"
  • "[Your Name]"
  • "[link to the revised article]"

8. Curly Quotes

LLMs (especially ChatGPT) use "curly quotes" and 'curly apostrophes' instead of straight quotes " and '. Use straight quotes unless your style guide specifically requires curly ones.

9. English Variety

Match the English variety to your context: - Writing about a British topic from India? Use British English. - LLMs default to American English. Humans don't always.

Quick Self-Check

Before publishing, ask:

  1. Did I use 3+ "AI vocabulary" words in any paragraph? → Replace them.
  2. Did I avoid "is/are" when simpler? → Simplify.
  3. Did I add "-ing" analysis phrases that don't add information? → Cut them.
  4. Did I inflate the subject's importance? → Deflate it.
  5. Are my headings in Title Case? → Change to sentence case.
  6. Did I bold too many terms? → Remove most bold.
  7. Are all my sources real and verifiable? → Check every one.
  8. Did I leave any placeholders? → Fill or remove them.
  9. Did I use Markdown instead of the target markup? → Convert.
  10. Does this sound like a press release or travel brochure? → Rewrite.

What NOT to Worry About

These are not reliable AI indicators: - Perfect grammar (many humans write well) - Formal tone (humans code-switch) - "Bland" or "robotic" prose (subjective) - Transition words like "Additionally" (in isolation) - Correct formatting (humans can learn markup) - Unsourced content (predates LLMs)
